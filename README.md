# Employee-Attrition-Analysis
Employee Attrition Analysis – Excel Dashboard
Project Overview

This project provides a detailed analysis of Employee Attrition using Excel Pivot Tables, Pivot Charts, and slicers.
The objective is to understand why employees leave, identify high-risk areas, and provide recommendations to improve retention.

The dashboard highlights key metrics across age, gender, job role, salary band, job satisfaction, performance rating, and work-life balance.

Objectives

Analyze patterns in employee attrition

Identify high-risk departments, roles, and salary bands

Understand factors influencing employee retention

Provide data-driven recommendations to reduce attrition

Present insights through an interactive Excel dashboard

Dataset Information

The dataset consists of 2,924 employees, including:

2,451 Current Employees

473 Ex-Employees

Key Columns:

Age, Gender

Department, Job Role

Monthly Income, Salary Hike %

Job Satisfaction, Work-Life Balance

Performance Rating

Years at Company

Total Working Years

Dashboard Features

This Excel Dashboard includes:

1) Pivot Tables for each analysis

2) Bar Charts & Column Charts

3) Line Charts for trends

4) Income Band Distribution

5) Attrition by Age, Gender, Salary, Job Role

6) Work-Life Balance & Job Satisfaction Insights

7) Years at Company Attrition Trend

8) Interactive Slicers for dynamic filtering

Each sheet provides a focused breakdown of different attrition factors.

Key Insights
 1. Salary Drives Attrition

Low-income employees have the highest attrition.

Higher salary bands show significantly better retention.

2. High-Risk Job Roles

Sales Executives, Lab Technicians, and Research Scientists have the highest exits.

Managerial positions show very low attrition.

3. Younger Employees Leave More

Highest attrition in 25–34 age group.

Employees under 25 also leave quickly due to early-career switching.

4. Poor Work-Life Balance Increases Attrition

WLB ratings 2 & 3 show maximum exits.

Employees with WLB 4 are the most stable.

5. Early Tenure Employees Are the Highest Risk

Most exits happen in the first 1–2 years.

Employees who stay past 3 years remain long-term.

Why Employees Are Leaving

Based on analysis across all charts:

Low Salary & Poor Salary Hike

High Work Pressure

Stressful job roles (Sales & R&D tech roles)

Lack of career clarity in early years

Low job satisfaction

Poor work-life balance

Limited recognition

 Recommendations
1. Improve Salary & Increment Structure

Increase base pay for low-income groups and provide better increments.

2. Strengthen Onboarding for 0–2 Year Employees

Mentorship, training plans, and regular check-ins.

3. Improve Work-Life Balance

Reduce overtime, allow flexible timings, rotate high-stress roles.

 4. Focus on Retention in High-Risk Roles

Sales & Lab roles need special programs, incentives, and workload adjustments.

5. Provide Learning & Career Growth Opportunities

Training, certifications, and clear promotion paths.

6. Recognize and Reward Employees

Regular appreciation programs to boost satisfaction.

 Conclusion

Employee attrition is not random—it follows clear patterns.
By improving compensation, onboarding, work-life balance, recognition, and career growth, the company can reduce attrition by 30–40%.

This dashboard provides actionable insights for HR and leadership teams to make better workforce decisions.
